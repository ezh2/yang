Benefits of fully utilizing vegetables...

The best way to increase nutritional value intake is to eat vegetables/fruits with peels. The most nutritious part of the vegetable, the peels, are rich in fiber, vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants. The risks of pesticides: By most accounts, the main purpose of removing peels is because peels contain the most pesticides. It’s rare to see restaurants or family cooks use every part of the vegetable, not only because of pesticides but the parts of vegetables are considered dirty and unable to wash. According to recent studies, 93% of the world population abandon vital nutritional parts of vegetables. This website will be informing and encouraging everyone to eat food with peels, and why you should. Moreover, teaching how to clean the peels, wash off pesticide residuals. All the vegetables of the following recipes on this website use vegetables from Taiwan. Taiwan is known for its abundance in agriculture fields, especially the production of a variety of vegetables. Taiwan has the Agricultural Chemicals and Toxic Substances Research Institute, established for the detection of pesticide residues in vegetables and fruits. The state-of-the-art in pesticide testing has reached the rate of random inspections of 98%. ​

The Vegetative Point

The vegetative point, also known as the vegetable growth point, which definition is the terminal cell of a leaf bud or stem from which new growth originates. In other words, it's the most nutritious part of the vegetable. It has the highest vitality, where a large amount of cell divides. For example, the core of cabbage will began forming new leafs, fibrous root of the burdock will dig into mud and start to grow.
Since the texture, the mouthfeel of the vegetative points are often rough and hard to bite, most people throw it away. However, the vegetative points are the most nutritious. It acts as a nutrition booster and a productive fertilizer. The anaerobic composting of food waste can be used for composting vegetative parts of the plants, if not needed. ​

The Truth of Pesticides


The Pesticide Paradox in Modern Agriculture:

Vegetables today are often associated with the term "poison" due to the prevalent use of pesticides. These chemicals and biological agents are essential in keeping pests away from our fruits and vegetables. The logic seems to be: the pricier the produce, the fewer the pesticides. However, organic farming, which avoids these chemicals, is the ideal alternative. In organic farmlands, it's common to see insects like dragonflies, frogs, and butterflies. These creatures thrive in environments free from pesticides. But the reality of business and societal demands means that many farmers, especially those with significant investments in their lands, feel compelled to use pesticides. This pressure has made pesticides a popular choice in agriculture, even though it means these lands no longer provide a suitable habitat for many insects. While small-scale organic farming is achievable, large-scale farms often find it challenging to avoid pesticides. One method to reduce their use is to grow a variety of crops together. This diversity can deter pests naturally. However, manual methods like hiring laborers to remove pests or using nets to cover crops are expensive. Spraying pesticides, although harmful and often criticized by consumers, becomes a more practical solution. It's a catch-22: consumers dislike pesticides but also demand crops free from pests.

The Impact of Pesticides and Mitigation:

The consequences of pesticide use are far-reaching. They can harm the human reproductive system, affecting sperm and egg cells that determine our future generations. In essence, our reliance on pesticides today could influence the health of our descendants. However, there are ways to minimize the effects of pesticides on our food. The preparation method, including the way vegetables are cut, can reduce the pesticide residue. For instance, cutting vegetables into smaller pieces can help wash away more pesticides. Some plants have natural defenses against pests. Tomatoes, for instance, have furry, sticky parts on their stems that deter insects. Ragile fruits, for example, strawberries, are most likely to be sprayed with pesticides. Pest loves the sweet and soft texture of the strawberry, making it precious so that pesticides are necessary. In conclusion, the agricultural sector is a in a dilemma. Both organic and pesticide-reliant farming have their challenges, and neither seems to fully satisfy societal demands. The most significant burden falls on the farmers, who must navigate these complexities while ensuring their livelihood.
